Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them

People with disabilities already face significant challenges in employment and education. Persons with disabilities face unemployment at more than three times higher levels than the rest of the population and suffer similar gaps in educational attainment. Yet 75% of people with disabilities who are not employed want to work. The main reason for this is not a lack of interest or education, but that the Internet is inherently unfriendly to many different kinds of disabilities. People of differing abilities obviously face different challenges in accessing the Internet. According to the Office for National Statistics, in May 2015, 27% of disabled adults had never used the internet, compared to 11% of non-disabled adults. The lack of alternative modalities for information on Web sites, for instance lack of alternative text that can be converted to audio to supplement visuals, or the lack of captions for audio and distracting visual or audio elements that cannot easily be turned off. Learning disabilities, depending on their nature, may face the same barriers, and the list goes on. As Technology evolves day by day, there is something readly positive available to almost anyone, anywhere and that includes people with disabilities in some cases. Many of today’s devices and platforms have various accessibility features built in. For example, iPhones have VoiceOver and speech options for people with impaired vision. They have hearing aid volume controls and flash features for those who are hearing impaired, and AssistiveTouch if you struggle with touching the screen. Websites like Facebook have accessibility options as well, which may come as a surprise to people who aren’t aware of them. For example, Facebook supports Voice Over and screen readers. Unit 2 (1.7)

Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites.

The most appropriate file type for websites are, the so-called the “big four” - JPG, GIF, PNG, and SVG but I will list a few more. JPG/ is 16-bit JPEG format (usually written without the E), and was designed with photographs in mind. It is capable of displaying millions of colours at once, without the need for dithering, allowing for the complex blend of hues that occur in photographic images. Then we have GIF, Graphics Interchange Format, Compuserve’s 8-bit GIF format has long been the most popular on the Internet, mainly because of its small size. It is ideal for small navigational icons and simple diagrams and illustrations where accuracy is required.GIFs can be animated, which is another reason they became so successful. Next in line is PNG (Portable Network Graphics) was invented in 1995 to replace the GIF and to get around some pesky patent issues. If the size of your file is not a pain point, and you’re working with a more complex image, PNG is probably the best choice. The latest of all the file types, SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics) is a vector image file format released in 2001, and is more powerful than other file formats suitable for the web. Unlike raster formats seen in JPG, GIF, and PNG, an SVG image remains crisp and clear at any resolution or size. That’s because an SVG is drawn from mathematically declared shapes and curves, not pixels. There are many more besides those mentioned above such as .HTML/ .HTM — HyperText Markup Language files, is a computer language devised to allow website creation. These websites can then be viewed by anyone else connected to the Internet. CSS — Cascading StyleSheet CSS files are a tool in the repertoire of webmasters that take care of how their websites look. MOV/ .QT — QuickTime MOVie was designed by Apple and originated on the Mac, but has made the transition to the PC and is hugely popular. That concludes the description of the most appropriate file types for websites.
